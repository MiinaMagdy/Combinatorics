Factorial

Definition

The factorial is defined for positive integers as n!=n(n1)21=i=1nin!=n \cdot (n-1) \cdots 2 \cdot 1 = \prod_{i=1}^n in!=n(n1)21=i=1ni. Alternatively, a recursive definition for the factorial is n!=n(n1)!n!=n \cdot (n-1)!n!=n(n1)!.

base case n=0n=0n=0 is defined as 0!=10!=10!=1.

The factorial is used in the definitions of combinations and permutations, as n!n!n! is the number of ways to order nnn distinct objects

Permutations

Definition

A permutation of nnn distinct objects is an ordering of these objects. The number of permutations of nnn distinct objects is n!n!n!. The number of permutations of nnn objects taken kkk at a time is n!(nk)!\frac{n!}{(n-k)!}(nk)!n!.

Formula

P(n,r)=n(n1)(n2)(nr+1)=n!(nr)!P(n,r)=n(n-1)(n-2)\cdots(n-r+1)=\frac{n!}{(n-r)!}P(n,r)=n(n1)(n2)(nr+1)=(nr)!n!.

Example

Elodie is putting on a fashion show and has five fabulous outfits for her five fabulous fashion models. However, on the day of the show, two of the outfits were ruined in an unfortunate permanent marker incident. Regardless, the show must go on and the remaining outfits will be presented. If each outfit can only be worn by one model and there is no time for any model to wear more than one dress, how many different shows can Elodie put on? (Note: Two shows are considered the same if they contain the same models wearing the same dresses.)

(A)120(B)60(C)720(D)5040(E)24(F)6\mathrm{(A)}\,120\quad\mathrm{(B)}\,60\quad\mathrm{(C)}\,720\quad\mathrm{(D)}\,5040\quad\mathrm{(E)}\,24\quad\mathrm{(F)}\,6(A)120(B)60(C)720(D)5040(E)24(F)6

Solution

Answer: (B)\mathrm{(B)}(B)

Since two of the outfits are ruined, we only have three outfits. There are five models available for the first outfit, four models available for the second outfit, and three models available for the third outfit. Therefore, there are 543=605 \cdot 4 \cdot 3 = \boxed{60}543=60 ways in which the models can be matched to the outfits.

Combinations

Definition

A combination, sometimes called a binomial coefficient, is a way of choosing rrr objects from a set of nnn where the order in which the objects are chosen is irrelevant. We are generally concerned with finding the number of combinations of size rrr from an original set of size nnn

Casework

Definition

casework is a counting method that involves splitting a problem into several parts, counting these parts individually, then adding together the totals of each part. Casework is a very general problem-solving approach, and as such has wide applicability.

Example

How many positive integers satisfy the equation x4+y<70x^4 + y < 70x4+y<70?

Solution

We use casework, based on the value of x.

Case 1: x=1x=1x=1. Then this problem becomes y<69y<69y<69, so there are 686868 possible values for yyy, and thus 686868 solutions when xxx is one.

Case 2: x=2x=2x=2. Then this problem becomes y<54y<54y<54, so there are 535353 possible values for yyy, and thus 535353 solutions when xxx is two.

If x=3x=3x=3, the problem becomes y<11y<-11y<11. But the question mandates that yyy is positive, so there are no solutions when y3y \geq 3y3. Thus, there are 68+53=12168+53=12168+53=121 positive integer solutions to the equation. \square

Complementary counting

Definition

complementary counting is a counting method where one counts what they don't want, then subtracts that from the total number of possibilities

Example

How many positive integers less than 100100100 are not a multiple of five?

Solution

We use a complementary approach. The total number of positive integers, with no restrictions, is 999999 integers. What we don't want are the multiples of five. These are 5,10,...,955, 10,..., 955,10,...,95 or 15,25,...,1951 \cdot 5, 2 \cdot 5,..., 19 \cdot 515,25,...,195; it's easy to see that there are 191919 of them. Thus, our answer is is 9919=8099-19 = 809919=80. \square

Pigeonhole Principle

Definition

the pigeonhole principle states that if n+1n+1n+1 or more pigeons are placed into nnn holes, one hole must contain two or more pigeons

Example

  1. If a Martian has an infinite number of red, blue, yellow, and black socks in a drawer, how many socks must the Martian pull out of the drawer to guarantee he has a pair?

    Solution

    The Martian must pull 5 socks out of the drawer to guarantee he has a pair. In this case the pigeons are the socks he pulls out and the holes are the colors. Thus, if he pulls out 5 socks, the Pigeonhole Principle states that some two of them have the same color. Also, note that it is possible to pull out 4 socks without obtaining a pair.

  1. Suppose SSS is a set of n+1n + 1n+1 integers. Prove that there exist distinct a,bSa, b \in Sa,bS such that aba - bab is a multiple of nnn.

    Solution

    Consider the residues of the elements of SSS, modulo nnn. By the Pigeonhole Principle, there exist distinct a,bSa, b \in Sa,bS such that ab(modn)a \equiv b \pmod nab(modn), as desired.

Example

Many states use a sequence of three letters followed by a sequence of three digits as their standard license-plate pattern. Given that each three-letter three-digit arrangement is equally likely, the probability that such a license plate will contain at least one palindrome (a three-letter arrangement or a three-digit arrangement that reads the same left-to-right as it does right-to-left) is mn\dfrac{m}{n}nm, where mmm and nnn are relatively prime positive integers. Find m+n.m+n.m+n.

Solution1

Consider the three-digit arrangement, aba\overline{aba}aba. There are 101010 choices for aaa and 101010 choices for bbb (since it is possible for a=ba=ba=b), and so the probability of picking the palindrome is 1010103=110\frac{10 \times 10}{10^3} = \frac 1{10}1031010=101. Similarly, there is a 126\frac 1{26}261 probability of picking the three-letter palindrome.

By the Principle of Inclusion-Exclusion, the total probability is

126+1101260=35260=7527+52=059\frac{1}{26}+\frac{1}{10}-\frac{1}{260}=\frac{35}{260}=\frac{7}{52}\quad\Longrightarrow\quad7+52=\boxed{059}261+1012601=26035=5277+52=059

Solution2

Using complementary counting, we count all of the license plates that do not have the desired property. In order to not be a palindrome, the first and third characters of each string must be different. Therefore, there are 1010910\cdot 10\cdot 910109 three digit non-palindromes, and there are 26262526\cdot 26\cdot 25262625 three letter non palindromes. As there are 10326310^3\cdot 26^3103263 total three-letter three-digit arrangements, the probability that a license plate does not have the desired property is 10109262625103263=4552\frac{10\cdot 10\cdot 9\cdot 26\cdot 26\cdot 25}{10^3\cdot 26^3}=\frac{45}{52}10326310109262625=5245. We subtract this from 1 to get 14552=7521-\frac{45}{52}=\frac{7}{52}15245=527 as our probability. Therefore, our answer is 7+52=0597+52=\boxed{059}7+52=059.
